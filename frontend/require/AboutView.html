About

PrairieLearn is an online system for adaptive problem-driven learning.

Purpose of PrairieLearn

PrairieLearn is designed to help you learn complex material quickly, and to give you accurate feedback about how successfully you can solve questions. This means that you will know how well prepared you are for exams, and you can target your study efforts effectively with good knowledge.

How it works

PrairieLearn uses machine learning algorithms to estimate your ability to get questions correct, which is shown as the Mastery score. If you get a hard question correct, then PrairieLearn will substantially upgrade its estimate of your ability (so you gain a lot of mastery), while getting an easy question wrong will cause it to substantially downgrade its view of you. This is just like what we do as humans, where we are impressed by people doing hard things correctly, but disappointed if they do easy things wrong.

PrairieLearn doesn't have a built-in idea of how “easy” or “hard” each question is. Instead, it adapts to your current ability level (your mastery score), in a form of adaptive learning. When PrairieLearn has a low opinion of your ability, then it will give you lots of mastery points for solving relatively simple questions. Later, when it thinks you are better, you will have to solve more complex questions to convince PrairieLearn of your abilities.

If you do a question several times in a row then PrairieLearn will stop paying attention to that question, because it isn't gaining much new information about your ability from seeing you do the same thing repeatedly. It will start to pay more attention (positive and negative) again to a question if you give it a break for a while by doing other questions.

Strategies for learning

You are free to use PrarieLearn in whatever way you prefer. To help guide you, the Recommended ratings for each question indicate which questions PrairieLearn thinks are an appropriate difficulty for you at your current level of demonstrated skill. Of course, it could be wrong about you, and so you are free to choose to do easier or harder questions as you like.

The questions are broadly arranged in order in each homework from simpler to more complex, so you should expect to generally progress through the questions as you study each week's topic. If you want to let PrairieLearn choose questions for you, then clicking the “Do a recommended question” button will give you questions that should be challenging but not impossible for you to do. PrairieLearn recommends questions that it thinks you have a roughly 50/50 chance of getting correct, and so are the best for learning.

When solving a question, it is best to work symbolically. This means that you define variables like $x$ or $v_r$ and work out the solution method with the variables, and then only substitute in the values at the very end. Although this might seem like more work up-front, it is very useful for actually getting the right answer.

PrairieLearn is making its best attempt to assess your mastery of each topic, so doing questions incorrectly will reduce its opinion of you and decrease your mastery score. This means that guessing is a bad idea, and you should not submit answers to questions unless you think you know how to do them. This lets you test your metacognition. This is “knowing about knowing”: do you know when you know how do solve a question, and do you know when you don't know? Good metacognition is essential for effective learning.

Technical details

The PrairieLearn algorithm is very similar to the machine learning methods that Netflix and Amazon use to recommend movies and products to you, based on your previous watching and purchasing history. The only difference is that PrairieLearn is judging your ability to solve problems, not your movie preferences.

For each question, PrairieLearn has an item response function that encodes the probability you will answer that question correctly, as a function of your current ability level. The exact model in PrairieLearn is a 4-parameter logistic (4PL) model, as used in item response theory.

PrairieLearn doesn't have a fixed idea of your ability, but rather has a Gaussian distribution that captures its belief in your skill. As you answer questions correctly or incorrectly, PrairieLearn uses Bayesian inference to update its belief model for your ability, which is then shown as your mastery score.

For any question, even ones you haven't done yet, PrairieLearn can use its question response functions and its belief in your ability to estimate how hard a question will be for you. This is used to generate its recommendations for you, as well as to compute the change in mastery that you will get for answering each question correctly or incorrectly.

Who makes PrairieLearn?

PrairieLearn is developed at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign in the Department of Mechanical Engineering. The PrairieLearn team is led by Prof. Matthew West.
